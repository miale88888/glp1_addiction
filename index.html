GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

The diabetes drug that keeps turning down the volume on craving

A structured, evidence-graded reading of what GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide, liraglutide, exenatide and newer dual agonists — actually do across four substance use disorders. Where the signal is real, where it is merely suggestive, and where it has so far failed.

Evidence through May 2026 Substances covered: Alcohol · Nicotine · Opioid · Stimulant Study types: RCT · Real-world cohort · Preclinical
No GLP-1 receptor agonist is approved by any regulator for a substance use disorder. Every use described here is investigational or off-label. This page summarizes published research for general information; it is not medical advice and is not a substitute for a clinician.

02

Why a gut hormone touches addiction

GLP-1 is a hormone released by the gut and brainstem after eating. Its receptors, it turns out, sit not only on the pancreas but throughout the brain's reward circuitry — which is why a drug built for blood sugar keeps showing up in addiction research.

Origin

Gut & brainstem release GLP-1

Endogenous GLP-1 signals satiety and regulates glucose. GLP-1 receptor agonists are engineered to mimic it and persist far longer in the body.

Relay — NTS

Nucleus tractus solitarius

The brainstem hub that receives gut signals and projects upward into the brain's motivation centers.

Reward core — VTA → NAc

Mesolimbic dopamine pathway

Agonism here blunts the dopamine surge that drugs, alcohol and nicotine hijack — dampening their reinforcing "pull." Animal work also implicates altered central GABA signaling.

Aversion gate — habenula

Lateral habenula

A separate circuit, especially relevant to nicotine, where GLP-1 signaling shapes how much of a substance feels like "enough."

Net behavioral effect

↓ craving · ↓ incentive salience · ↓ consumption

Across species, the consistent readout is reduced wanting and intake — not a blockade, but a turning-down of the reward's grip.

The leading hypothesis is that these drugs reduce the incentive salience of a reward — how much it commands attention and effort — rather than blocking pleasure outright.

This shared mechanism is exactly why the same molecule shows up in alcohol, nicotine and opioid research at once: addictive substances converge on overlapping reward circuitry, and GLP-1 signaling appears to act upstream of that convergence.

It also predicts the pattern we see in the data — a robust effect on appetitive, dopamine-heavy behaviors (drinking, eating, smoking) and a weaker, less consistent effect where the pharmacology is different (stimulants).

03

Substance by substance

The same drug class, four separate evidence bases. Below, what each one has actually shown in humans — and the honest gaps.

Alcohol use disorder

The flagship indication. Two randomized trials in 2025–2026 converted a decade of preclinical and real-world hints into genuine clinical evidence.

Strongest signal · 9/10
What the evidence shows
  • SEMALCO RCT (Lancet, 2026) — 108 adults with moderate–severe AUD plus obesity. Semaglutide 2.4 mg + CBT cut heavy-drinking days by 13.7 percentage points more than placebo (p=0.0015), with moderate effect sizes and a number-needed-to-treat of ~4.3 — favorable versus existing approved medications.
  • Hendershot RCT (JAMA Psychiatry, 2025) — a phase-2 trial in 48 non-treatment-seeking adults. Even at low doses over 9 weeks, semaglutide reduced weekly alcohol intake, cravings and heavy-drinking days; smokers in the trial also cut cigarettes per day.
  • Real-world cohort (Nature Communications, 2024) — among 83,825 patients with obesity, semaglutide was linked to a 50–56% lower rate of both new and recurrent AUD diagnoses versus other anti-obesity drugs; the pattern held in ~600,000 patients with diabetes.
  • Earlier exenatide RCT (2022) — no overall effect on drinking, but reduced heavy drinking and brain cue-reactivity in the subgroup with obesity — an early hint that body weight and drug generation matter.

What we still don't know

Both RCTs are modest in size and short in duration; the larger one required comorbid obesity, so effects in normal-weight drinkers are unproven. Durability after stopping, and whether lifelong dosing is needed, remain open. A definitive phase-3 trial is only now beginning.

Nicotine / tobacco use disorder

A real but narrower benefit: the clearest effect so far is on the weight gain that drives so many people back to smoking, with a more modest effect on quitting itself.

Mixed / modest · 6/10
What the evidence shows
  • Post-cessation weight gain is the most consistent win. In a pooled analysis of three RCTs (410 participants), people who quit while on a GLP-1 agonist lost weight while controls gained — removing a major relapse trigger.
  • Cessation itself shows a smaller, less consistent benefit. Exenatide trials found stronger effects in heavier smokers and those without prediabetes or obesity; one dulaglutide + varenicline subgroup showed no added benefit.
  • Real-world diabetes data — among ~223,000 patients with tobacco use disorder, semaglutide was associated with 12–30% fewer tobacco-disorder diagnoses and 40–70% fewer prescriptions for cessation aids, with effects emerging within the first month.
  • Mechanism uniquely implicates the lateral habenula in controlling nicotine intake, alongside the shared reward pathway.

Long-term abstinence rates remain modest, and cravings plus weight rebound can return after stopping. Dedicated, adequately powered cessation trials with newer agents are still in progress rather than complete.

Opioid use disorder

Striking observational signals on overdose risk — but, crucially, almost no randomized human data yet. This is a promise, not a proof.

Emerging · 4/10
What the evidence shows
  • Overdose protection (Addiction, 2025) — across 503,747 patients with OUD, a GLP-1 / GIP prescription was associated with a ~40% lower rate of opioid overdose (adjusted IRR 0.60). In the same dataset, AUD patients had ~50% lower alcohol-intoxication rates.
  • Target-trial emulation (JAMA Network Open, 2024) — in patients with both diabetes and OUD, semaglutide was linked to roughly halved opioid-overdose risk versus other antidiabetic drugs.
  • Early clinical & preclinical — a small pilot reported reduced opioid craving; in rodents, liraglutide cut cue- and drug-induced heroin seeking.
  • A randomized trial is now testing semaglutide added to standard medications (buprenorphine or methadone) for OUD.

What we still don't know

These are associations, not causation: people prescribed GLP-1 drugs differ systematically from those who aren't, and residual confounding is hard to rule out. Until the randomized trials report, opioid use should be considered an open question — not an established use.

Stimulant (cocaine) use disorder

The honest outlier. The one controlled human test was negative, and the pharmacology gives a plausible reason why.

Inconclusive · 2/10
What the evidence shows
  • The one human trial was negative. A double-blind, within-subject study gave a single acute dose of exenatide to people with cocaine use disorder and saw no change in cocaine intake, wanting, or self-reported euphoria.
  • Major caveats — it used a single dose of an older-generation agent, far from the sustained, high-dose regimens that work elsewhere. It cannot rule out a benefit from modern agents given properly.
  • Why stimulants may differ — cocaine acts very directly on dopamine reuptake, a different pharmacological lever than the appetitive, satiety-linked rewards where GLP-1 drugs perform best.
  • Some preclinical work hints at effects on stimulant reward, but it has not translated to humans.

What we still don't know

Essentially everything. Whether semaglutide or tirzepatide, dosed to steady state, could do anything for stimulant use is untested. For now the responsible reading is "no demonstrated benefit," not "ruled out."

05

Read this before you get excited

The honest counterweight. Promising is not the same as proven, and most of the eye-catching numbers above come with serious asterisks.

01

Observational ≠ causal

The biggest numbers come from health-record studies. People prescribed GLP-1 drugs differ from those who aren't, and no statistical adjustment fully removes that bias.

02

Small, short RCTs

The strongest randomized evidence — in alcohol — rests on trials of ~50–110 people over a few months. None is a definitive phase-3 study, and most required comorbid obesity.

03

Zero approvals

No regulator has approved any of these drugs for any addiction. All clinical use is off-label, without dosing or safety data specific to this purpose.

04

Side effects & lean mass

Nausea and other GI effects are common, and muscle-mass loss is a growing concern — especially in people who are not overweight to begin with.

05

Durability unknown

Benefits may fade once the drug stops, raising the prospect of indefinite treatment for a chronic condition — with all the cost and access problems that implies.

06

Cost & equity

These medicines are expensive and frequently in short supply. Even if trials succeed, who can actually obtain them is a separate, unsolved question.

06

What's coming next

The field is moving from "interesting signal" to "test it properly." A handful of trials over the next few years will decide whether any of this becomes real medicine.

Phase-3 AUD trial in veterans

Phase 3

The first large, definitive randomized test of semaglutide 2.4 mg for moderate–severe alcohol use disorder, run by the U.S. VA. The result most likely to settle the alcohol question.

NCT07218354 · ~2026 start · est. completion 2029

Semaglutide added to OUD medication

Phase 2

Tests whether semaglutide on top of buprenorphine or methadone reduces illicit opioid use — the first proper randomized look at the overdose signal.

NCT06548490 · semaglutide + MOUD

Oral semaglutide (Rybelsus) for AUD

Completed

A randomized trial of the daily oral formulation in adults with alcohol use disorder; results will broaden the dosing picture beyond injections.

NCT05892432 · completed 2025

Smoking-cessation RCTs with fMRI

Recruiting

Trials pairing GLP-1 agonists with objective measures — exhaled CO, brain imaging — to pin down whether the cessation benefit is real and mechanistic.

Multiple registrations · ongoing
